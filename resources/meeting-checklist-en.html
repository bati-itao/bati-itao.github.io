Accessible Events / Meetings Checklist

Prior to meeting/event

  • Accessible by Design from the start: meeting or event planners are encouraged to consider accessibility from the start, and not implement it after the fact.
  • In the meeting invite always indicate that employees who require accommodations: Sign Language Interpreters (SLI), Communication Access Real-time Translation (CART - the instant translation of spoken word into displayed text), large fonts, etc., should contact the organizer as soon as possible in order to ensure availability of services. A deadline should be provided for such requests to be sent to the organizer to ensure accommodations can be secured.
    • If participants do require accommodations, follow-up to confirm if they will be providing their own or if they would like you to arrange services. Ask if they have a preferred service provider.
  • Indicate how participants’ confidential information will be handled, who will have access this information, and provide a link to the privacy policy on accommodation needs.
    • For example: Any and all accommodations requested will be kept confidential and only used for the purposes of procuring accessibility services.
  • For an open-door type meeting where you have no way of knowing the needs of your participants, then you should provide as many accommodations as possible, including but not limited to: simultaneous English/French translation, CART, sign language interpreters in both American Sign Language (ASL - English) and Langue des signes québécoise (LSQ - French). You will need to secure official language interpretation (English – French) if you expect to have both languages spoken during the event.
    • Please see Annex A for additional information regarding CART services and SLI.
    • Note that adding multiple accommodation features may increase technical complexity (e.g. need for multiple channels for simultaneous interpretation), and what accommodates one person may create a barrier to someone else, so organizers should engage early with IITB if special accommodations are required.
  • Ensure all meeting-related emails, documents, and presentations are accessible and sent to participants at least one week prior to the meeting.
    • Consult Accessible Documents - ESDC / IT Accessibility Office for additional information on creating accessible documents.
  • Send unilingual documents/presentations (one in English and one in French) one week prior to participants and to all service providers. The presentations should also include the translated speaker notes as well as the alternative text.
    • See Annex B for additional information regarding Official Languages recommendations.
  • Schedule health breaks for longer meetings and ensure they occur on time.
    • These are helpful for participants that may need to take medication, have a break for their service dog, self-care, and more.
  • Provide multiple methods to join the meeting such as dial-in, web, and mobile devices.
  • Do a “Dry Run”, practice meeting a couple of days prior the event to ensure everything runs smoothly.
  • On the day of the event, about one hour before the start time, ask all interpreters and captionists to join the event to ensure no technical issues with login and to ensure everything is working.

Conducting the meeting

  • Foster an inclusive and safe space by encouraging disability-safe language during meetings by raising awareness of ableist language and preferred terminology.
    • Avoid phrases such as, “turning a blind eye to…”, “being deaf to…”.
    • Encourage phrases such as “person with a disability” and not “handicapped”, or “wheelchair-bound”, etc.
  • Introduce interpreters and/or captioners.
  • Ensure participants are muted.
  • Discourage cross-talk among participants. Only one person should be speaking at a time, allowing for clear communication.
  • Encourage participants to use plain language.
  • When possible and with the consent of all participants, set the meeting to be recorded in order to allow participants to review the presentation and take notes at their own pace.
    • Notify the participants on how and when the recording will be shared with them.
  • Include the link to the captioning service in the chat at the beginning of the event, and again about ten minutes later. The link can also be sent by email to participants, and included in the invitation.
  • Remind participants how to pin interpreters’ videos and speakers if needed.
  • Mention to participants to use the chat only for questions, as it can become overwhelming and distracting for some to have too many chat messages.
  • Read relevant Chat messages aloud and you can also ask participants to use the Raise your hand feature to ask questions, and continue to have someone monitor participants in case someone is waving on camera.
    • For those who cannot use these methods, provide other ways of participating.
  • Describe all content of the visual material (such as PowerPoints and screen shares)
    • When using MS Teams, encourage presenters to use the Share PowerPoint feature instead of just sharing their screen to allow assistive technology users to engage with the PowerPoint.
  • Remind participants of in-event etiquette:
    • Log out of VPN
    • Stay on mute when not speaking
    • Keep camera off if not presenting, to reduce visual stimuli except the presenter
    • Turn your camera on when speaking, to allow others who lip read to do so.
      • Some participants may have anxiety or other accommodation needs that would required them to not be on camera. If this is the case, if the participant feels comfortable, they can turn on their camera, else ensure that their message continues to be clearly communicated to all other participants.
  • Send relevant information and any links shared in the Chat by email after the meeting.
  • Strive to end the meeting on time to respect participants’ schedules and time.

Best practices for presenters

  • Ensure that presenters have consulted the Planning Inclusive and Accessible Events document, the section on tips for presenters.

Remind presenters to:

  • Use plain language and explain all terms and acronyms.
  • Respect their allotted time, to ensure health breaks and the meeting ends as scheduled.
  • Announce their name before speaking.
  • Speak at a moderate pace and enunciate clearly.
  • Find a quiet environment to avoid noise distractions.
  • Use a plain and non-distracting background.
  • Use adequate lighting when sharing video for individuals who lip read.
  • Ensure to face your camera when talking for individuals who lip read.
