Contrast: Make text and visuals easy to see

Strong contrast makes text, icons, and links easier to read for everyone—especially people with low vision, anyone on small screens, and in bright rooms or on projectors.

Quick checklist (at a glance)

  • Body text is dark on light (or light on dark)—no pale grey on white.
  • Links are clearly different from body text (underline or strong visual cue).
  • No text over busy images; if unavoidable, add a solid or shaded box behind the text.
  • Callouts, table headers, and captions all have clear contrast.
  • Charts use colours that are easy to tell apart and another cue (labels/patterns/markers).
  • You can still read everything on a bright display or projector.
  • A contrast checker shows at least 4.5:1 for normal text and 3:1 for large text.

Core practices

Text and links

  • Use dark text on light backgrounds (or the reverse). Avoid pale text.
  • Make links stand out: underline or use a strong visual style (not just colour).
  • Avoid very thin fonts for small text; pick a readable weight and size.

Backgrounds and images

  • Do not place text on busy photos or patterns. If you must, place the text in a solid or shaded box.
  • Avoid watermarks/patterns behind body text.
  • Check callout boxes, captions, and table headers—not just body text.

Charts and data

  • Choose colours that are easy to distinguish.
  • Don’t rely on colour alone. Add labels, patterns, or distinct marker shapes.
  • Include a simple data table when people need exact values.

How to check and improve contrast (step-by-step)

Using Microsoft 365's built-in accessibility checker

  1. In Word, PowerPoint, or Excel, go to the Review tab.
  2. Click Check Accessibility.
  3. Look for warnings about "Hard-to-read text contrast".
  4. Click on any contrast issues in the results panel.
  5. Follow the suggested fixes to improve contrast.

Fixing common issues

Text colour

  1. Select the text → Home → Font Color.
  2. Darken text on light backgrounds, or lighten text on dark backgrounds.
  3. Avoid grey on white for body text.
  4. Quick test: can someone read it from across the room?

Backgrounds and images

  1. If text sits on an image, add a solid background (e.g., Insert → Shapes for a text box).
  2. Or use Text Highlight Color to add shading behind text.
  3. Pick background colours that create strong contrast with your text.

Link visibility

  1. Select your link text.
  2. Ensure links are underlined (Home → Underline).
  3. Pick link colours that stand out from body text.
  4. Quick test: can you spot links when scanning the page?

Online contrast checking tools

  1. Use the Colour Contrast Analyser tool (available from the National Service Desk Application Catalogue).
  2. Sample your text colour and background colour with the picker.
  3. Target ratios: 4.5:1 (normal text), 3:1 (large text—18 pt or 14 pt bold).
  4. Adjust colours until you meet or exceed the ratio.

Verification Reminder: Always verify contrast with a checker suited to your font weight/size and background context.

PowerPoint Tip: Set colours in the Slide Master so contrast stays consistent across slides.

Creating accessible colour schemes

Good combinations

  • Dark blue (#000080) on white
  • Black (#000000) on white
  • White on dark blue (#000080)
  • Yellow (#FFFF00) on black (high visibility; use sparingly)

Poor combinations

  • Red text on green backgrounds
  • Light grey text on white
  • Blue links on purple backgrounds
  • Any text directly on busy images

Validate and maintain

  • Real-world check: View on a laptop and a bright projector. Still easy to read?
  • Scan the set: Headings, body text, links, callouts, table headers—do they all stand out?
  • Peer review: Ask a colleague to flag anything hard to see.
  • Large text rule: ≥18 pt regular or 14 pt bold needs only 3:1 contrast; all other text needs 4.5:1.
