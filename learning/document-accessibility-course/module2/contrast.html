Contrast: Make text and visuals easy to see

Strong contrast makes text, icons, and links easier to read for everyone. It helps people with low vision, people viewing on small screens, and people reading in bright rooms or on projectors.

Make text and links clear

  • Use dark text on a light background, or light text on a dark background. Avoid pale text.
  • Make links stand out from body text. Use an underline or another strong visual cue.
  • Avoid very thin fonts for small text. Choose a weight and size that is easy to read.

Backgrounds and images

  • Do not place text on top of busy images. If you must, add a solid or shaded box behind the text.
  • Avoid watermarks or patterns behind body text.
  • Check callout boxes and table headers, not just body text. Make sure the text colour stands out.

Charts and data

  • Pick colours that are easy to tell apart.
  • Don't use color alone for meaning. Add labels, patterns, or different marker shapes.
  • Include a simple data table when it helps people read the values.

Quick check

  • Look at your document on a laptop and on a projector or bright screen. Is everything still easy to read?
  • Scan headings, body text, links, callouts, and table headers. Do they stand out from the background?
  • Ask a colleague to do a quick read and tell you what is hard to see.

How to check and improve contrast step-by-step

Follow these practical steps to ensure your documents have adequate contrast for all users:

Using Microsoft 365's built-in accessibility checker

  1. In Word, PowerPoint, or Excel, go to the Review tab
  2. Click Check Accessibility
  3. Look for warnings about "Hard-to-read text contrast"
  4. Click on any contrast issues in the results panel
  5. Follow the suggested fixes to improve contrast

Fixing common contrast problems

Text color issues

  1. Select the problematic text
  2. Go to Home tab → Font Color dropdown
  3. Choose a darker color for light backgrounds, or lighter color for dark backgrounds
  4. Avoid gray text on white backgrounds
  5. Test: Can someone across the room read it?

Background and image issues

  1. Select text that's hard to read over images
  2. Go to Home → Text Highlight Color
  3. Add a solid background color, or
  4. Use Insert → Shapes to add a text box with solid background
  5. Choose backgrounds that create strong contrast with your text

Link visibility issues

  1. Select your link text
  2. Make sure links are underlined (go to Home → Underline )
  3. Choose link colors that stand out from body text
  4. Test: Can you easily spot links when scanning the page?

Online contrast checking tools

For precise contrast checking, you can use these free tools:

  1. Use the Colour Contrast Analyser tool (available from the National Service Desk Application Catalogue)
  2. Use the color picker to test your text and background colors
  3. Aim for at least 4.5:1 ratio for normal text (11 pt or more), 3:1 for large text (18 point or more)
  4. Make adjustments based on the results

Avoid problematic combinations

  • Red text on green backgrounds
  • Light gray text on white
  • Blue links on purple backgrounds
  • Any text directly on busy images
Test with different users:

Ask colleagues with different vision abilities to review your document

Verification Reminder: Always verify contrast with a checker appropriate to your font weight/size and background context.

PowerPoint Tip: Prefer setting contrast at the slide/layout level via the PowerPoint Slide Master for consistency.
