What is the Accessibility Assistant

The Accessibility Assistant is a built-in feature in Microsoft 365 applications that provides real-time guidance to help you create more accessible documents. It works like a helpful colleague looking over your shoulder, offering suggestions and tips as you work to ensure your content can be used by everyone, including people with disabilities.

The Accessibility Assistant provides proactive guidance throughout your document creation process, helping you make accessible choices from the start.

What does it do

The Accessibility Assistant helps you by:

  • Providing real-time suggestions: Offers immediate feedback as you add content, helping prevent accessibility issues before they occur
  • Explaining accessibility concepts: Gives clear, plain-language explanations of why certain practices matter for accessibility
  • Suggesting improvements: Recommends specific actions to make your content more accessible
  • Teaching best practices: Helps you learn accessibility principles through practical, contextual guidance
  • Saving time: Reduces the need for extensive reviews and fixes by catching issues early

How it works

The Accessibility Assistant uses intelligent technology to analyze your content as you create it. It watches for common accessibility challenges and provides timely, relevant suggestions.

Real-time analysis

As you work, the Assistant continuously scans your document for potential accessibility issues. When it detects something that could be improved, it provides a gentle notification or suggestion.

Contextual guidance

The Assistant provides suggestions that are relevant to what you're currently doing. For example, when you insert an image, it might remind you to add alternative text.

Learning-focused approach

Rather than just pointing out problems, the Assistant explains why accessibility matters and how your choices impact users with disabilities.

How to use the Accessibility Assistant

The Accessibility Assistant is designed to be unobtrusive while still being helpful. Here's how to make the most of it:

Accessing the Accessibility Assistant

To access accessibility checking features:

  1. In Word/PowerPoint/Excel (Desktop): Go to Review > Check Accessibility
  2. If the Accessibility Assistant is available in your tenant/build, it will appear in a right pane
  3. Otherwise, the classic Accessibility Checker opens

Note: Features and UI vary by app, version, and tenant. The exact location and available features may differ between applications and versions.

Working with suggestions

When the Assistant provides a suggestion:

  • Read the suggestion carefully: Understand why the change is recommended
  • Apply the suggestion: Make the recommended change if it makes sense for your content
  • Learn from the feedback: Use suggestions as learning opportunities for future documents
  • Dismiss when appropriate: Not all suggestions may apply to your specific context
Customizing your experience

Depending on your version and tenant configuration, you may be able to adjust how the Accessibility Assistant works:

  • Notification frequency: Choose how often you want to see suggestions (when available)
  • Types of suggestions: Select which accessibility areas you want help with (when available)
  • Learning level: Adjust the detail level of explanations based on your experience (when available)

Note: Features and UI vary by app, version, and tenant. Not all customization options may be available in your environment.

Accessibility Assistant across applications

While the core function remains the same, the Accessibility Assistant adapts to each Microsoft 365 application:

Microsoft Word

In Word, the Assistant helps with:

  • Heading structure and navigation
  • Alternative text for images and graphics
  • Table accessibility (headers, captions)
  • Color contrast and text formatting
  • Hyperlink text clarity
  • Document language settings
Microsoft PowerPoint

In PowerPoint, the Assistant focuses on:

  • Slide reading order and structure
  • Alternative text for images, charts, and shapes
  • Color contrast in slides
  • Font size and readability
  • Slide title clarity
  • Animation and transition accessibility
Microsoft Excel

In Excel, the Assistant assists with:

  • Table headers and data structure
  • Chart and graph accessibility
  • Cell naming and organization
  • Color coding alternatives
  • Worksheet navigation
  • Formula accessibility
Microsoft Outlook

In Outlook, the Assistant helps with:

  • Email subject line clarity
  • Message structure and headings
  • Alternative text for embedded images
  • Link text descriptiveness
  • Color contrast in emails
  • Attachment accessibility

Benefits of using the Assistant

  • Prevention over correction: Catch accessibility issues before they become problems
  • Continuous learning: Improve your accessibility knowledge through practical application
  • Time savings: Reduce the time spent on accessibility reviews and corrections
  • Consistent quality: Maintain accessibility standards across all your documents
  • User empathy: Better understand how your choices affect users with disabilities
  • Confidence building: Feel more confident that your documents are accessible

Tips for effective use

Start with the Assistant enabled

Turn on the Accessibility Assistant before you begin creating new documents. This helps you develop good habits from the start rather than trying to fix issues later.

Don't ignore suggestions

While you don't need to accept every suggestion, take time to understand why each recommendation is made. This understanding will help you make better choices in the future.

Use it as a learning tool

Pay attention to the explanations provided with suggestions. The Assistant is designed to teach accessibility principles, not just point out problems.

Share knowledge with your team

When you learn something new from the Assistant, share that knowledge with colleagues. This helps build accessibility awareness across your organization.
