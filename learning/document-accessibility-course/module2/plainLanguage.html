Plain language principles

  • Use short sentences and common words.
  • Use active voice and direct instructions.
  • Front-load key information and avoid jargon.
  • Break up dense text with headings and lists.

Meaningful link text

  • Write link text that describes the destination or action.
  • Avoid “click here” or “read more.”
  • Include file type/size where relevant (e.g., “Annual Report (PDF, 2 MB)”).

Structure for understanding

  • Use headings (H1–H3) to organize ideas logically.
  • Use lists for steps and sets of related items.
  • Define terms when you first use them.

Test with real users and tools

  • Run a readability check; aim for a reasonable grade level for the audience.
  • Ask peers to scan and summarize your content—if they can’t, refine it.

How to write in plain language step-by-step

Follow these practical steps to transform complex writing into clear, accessible content:

Planning your content

  1. Know your audience:
    • Consider their reading level and familiarity with your topic
    • Think about when and how they'll use this information
    • Ask: What do they need to know or do?
  2. Identify your main message:
    • Write down the 3 most important points
    • Put the most important information first
    • Save supporting details for later sections

Writing clear sentences

  1. Use active voice:
    • Passive: "The form must be completed by the employee"
    • Active: "The employee must complete the form"
    • Tip: Look for "by" phrases and rearrange
  2. Keep sentences short:
    • Aim for 15-20 words per sentence
    • Break long sentences at natural points
    • Use periods instead of semicolons when possible
  3. Put the main idea first:
    • Weak: "After reviewing your application and checking your eligibility, we will send you a decision"
    • Strong: "We will send you a decision after we review your application"

Choosing simple words

  1. Replace complex words with simple ones:
    • "Use" instead of "utilize"
    • "Help" instead of "assist"
    • "About" instead of "regarding"
    • "Show" instead of "demonstrate"
  2. Define technical terms when needed:
    • First use: "API (Application Programming Interface)"
    • Or explain: "The API, which connects different software programs..."
    • Consider if the technical term is really necessary
  3. Avoid unnecessary jargon:
    • Instead of "leverage," say "use"
    • Instead of "facilitate," say "help"
    • Instead of "subsequent to," say "after"

Organizing information clearly

  1. Use meaningful headings:
    • Weak: "Information"
    • Strong: "How to apply for benefits"
    • Make headings scannable and specific
  2. Break up long paragraphs:
    • Aim for 3-5 sentences per paragraph
    • One main idea per paragraph
    • Use white space to help readers
  3. Use lists for steps or related items:
    • Numbered lists for processes (do this, then this)
    • Bulleted lists for related items
    • Keep list items parallel in structure

Writing helpful instructions

  1. Use action words (verbs):
    • "Click Submit" not "Submission of the form"
    • "Call us" not "Contact may be made"
    • Start instructions with action words
  2. Be specific about what to do:
    • Weak: "Provide necessary documentation"
    • Strong: "Send us a copy of your birth certificate"
    • Include details that help people succeed
  3. Address the reader directly:
    • Use "you" instead of "the applicant"
    • Use "we" instead of "the department"
    • This creates a more conversational tone

Using Microsoft Word's readability tools

  1. Turn on readability statistics:
    • Go to File → Options → Proofing
    • Check "Show readability statistics"
    • Run spelling and grammar check to see scores
  2. Understanding the scores:
    • Flesch Reading Ease: Higher is better (60-70 is good for most audiences)
    • Grade Level: Lower is better (aim for 8th-10th grade for general audiences)
    • Use these as guides, not absolute rules
  3. Improving your scores:
    • Shorten long sentences
    • Replace complex words with simpler ones
    • Break up long paragraphs

Editing for plain language

  1. Read aloud:
    • Read your draft out loud
    • Mark places where you stumble or need to take a breath
    • These often need shorter sentences
  2. Look for warning signs:
    • Sentences longer than 2 lines
    • Paragraphs longer than 5 sentences
    • Words ending in -tion, -ment, -ness (often can be simplified)
  3. Test with real people:
    • Ask colleagues to read and summarize key points
    • Note where they get confused or ask questions
    • Revise those sections for clarity

Common plain language edits

  1. Before and after examples:
    • Before: "In order to facilitate the processing of your application..."
    • After: "To process your application..."
  2. More examples:
    • Before: "It is imperative that you"
    • After: "You must"
  3. Even more:
    • Before: "In the event that"
    • After: "If"

Quick plain language checklist

  1. ✓ Most sentences are 20 words or fewer
  2. ✓ Paragraphs focus on one main idea
  3. ✓ Headings clearly describe what follows
  4. ✓ Instructions use action words (verbs)
  5. ✓ Technical terms are defined when necessary
  6. ✓ The most important information comes first
  7. ✓ A colleague can read and understand the main points
