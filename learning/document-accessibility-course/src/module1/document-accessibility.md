Document accessibility is about making digital documents—such as PDFs, Word files, and presentations—accessible to people with disabilities. This means that users can perceive, understand, navigate, and interact with the content using assistive technologies.

Tim Berners-Lee, W3C (World Wide Web Consortium) Director and inventor of the World Wide Web, emphasized that "The power of the Web is in its universality. Access by everyone regardless of disability is an essential aspect." This principle applies equally to digital documents.

Document accessibility addresses all disabilities that affect access, including visual, auditory, physical, speech, cognitive, and neurological. Accessible documents use proper structure, headings, alt text for images, readable fonts, and logical reading order.

Document accessibility also benefits people without disabilities—for example, users on mobile devices, individuals with temporary impairments or situational limitations, older adults, and those using limited or slower technology. Accessible documents improve usability and inclusivity for all.
