Avoiding essential content in Headers and Footers

What it is

Headers and footers in Word are special sections that appear at the top or bottom of every page (or specific pages) in your document. They typically contain information like page numbers, document titles, dates, or logos. While they're useful for consistent page layout, they present unique accessibility challenges.

In Word, headers and footers are:

  • Separate from the main document content
  • Accessed through a special editing mode (double-click to open)
  • Repeated across multiple pages automatically
  • Often visually separated from the main content

Why it matters

Headers and footers create several accessibility problems:

  • Reading order issues: Screen readers may not read headers and footers in the expected order relative to main content, or may skip them entirely depending on settings
  • Navigation difficulties: Users may have trouble finding content placed in headers/footers since it's outside the normal document flow
  • PDF conversion problems: When Word documents are converted to PDF, header/footer content may not be properly tagged or may appear in unexpected locations in the reading order
  • Mobile and reflow issues: Content in headers and footers may not display properly on mobile devices or when users need to reflow text

❌ Don't put essential content in headers and footers

Never place the following in headers or footers:

  • Contact information needed to reach someone
  • Important disclaimers or legal notices
  • Critical instructions or warnings
  • Content references or citations
  • Navigation links or menus

How to do it

What you CAN put in headers and footers

It's acceptable to use headers and footers for:

  • Page numbers: Automatic page numbering
  • Document metadata: Document title, creation date, version number
  • Non-essential visual elements: Decorative logos or branding (with proper alt text)
  • Section markers: Chapter or section names for navigation reference

Best practices for header and footer content

  1. Keep it minimal: Only include supplementary information that enhances but isn't essential to understanding the document
  2. Make it descriptive but brief: If you use headers/footers for document titles or section names, keep them concise
  3. Include alt text for images: Any logos or images in headers/footers need proper alt text, or should be marked as decorative if not essential
  4. Test the reading order: Use a screen reader or the accessibility checker to verify header/footer content doesn't disrupt document flow
  5. Duplicate essential information: If you have important content in a header or footer, also include it in the main document body

✅ Good practice example

Header contains: Document title "Annual Report 2024" and page numbers

Footer contains: Company logo (marked decorative) and document version "v1.2"

Main document contains: All essential content, instructions, and contact information

How to edit headers and footers

  1. Double-click in the header or footer area to open editing mode
  2. Or go to Insert tab → Header or Footer
  3. Make your changes keeping accessibility in mind
  4. Click Close Header and Footer or press Esc to return to the main document

Using Navigation Pane for heading validation

What it is

The Navigation Pane in Word is a powerful tool that displays your document's structure based on heading styles. It shows a hierarchical outline of all headings in your document, allowing you to see at a glance how your content is organized and navigate quickly between sections.

The Navigation Pane can display:

  • Headings (hierarchical list based on Heading 1, 2, 3, etc.)
  • Pages (thumbnail view of document pages)
  • Results (when using the search function)

Why it matters

The Navigation Pane is crucial for accessibility because:

  • Mirrors screen reader experience: The heading list in the Navigation Pane is similar to what screen reader users experience when navigating by headings
  • Validates structure: You can immediately see if your heading hierarchy is logical and complete
  • Identifies problems: Missing headings, skipped levels, or improperly formatted text become obvious
  • Tests navigation: If you can't easily find content using the Navigation Pane, screen reader users will have the same difficulty
  • Improves your workflow: Makes it easier to organize and navigate your own documents

The Navigation Pane test

If someone could understand your document's organization and find specific information using only the Navigation Pane (without reading the full text), your document structure is likely accessible.

How to do it

Opening the Navigation Pane

There are several ways to open the Navigation Pane:

  • Go to View tab → check Navigation Pane in the Show group
  • Press Ctrl+F (opens Navigation Pane in search mode)
  • Or use keyboard shortcut Alt, W, K

Using the Navigation Pane to validate headings

  1. Open the Navigation Pane and click the Headings tab (leftmost icon that looks like a document)
  2. Review the heading structure:
    • Is there a single Heading 1 at the top (document title)?
    • Do Heading 2s represent main sections?
    • Are Heading 3s nested under appropriate Heading 2s?
    • Are there any skipped levels (e.g., Heading 1 to Heading 3)?
  3. Check heading text:
    • Are headings descriptive and meaningful?
    • Can you understand the document structure from headings alone?
    • Are there any missing sections?
  4. Test navigation: Click on different headings to jump to those sections. Does this provide an efficient way to move through the document?
  5. Look for problems:
    • Text that looks like a heading but doesn't appear (it's manually formatted, not using styles)
    • Headings appearing at wrong levels
    • Too many or too few headings

✅ Good heading structure in Navigation Pane

Annual Report 2024 (Heading 1)
  Executive Summary (Heading 2)
  Financial Performance (Heading 2)
    Revenue Growth (Heading 3)
    Expense Management (Heading 3)
      Operating Costs (Heading 4)
      Capital Investments (Heading 4)
  Future Outlook (Heading 2)
  Appendices (Heading 2)
    Appendix A: Financial Statements (Heading 3)
    Appendix B: Methodology (Heading 3)

Common problems you'll spot with Navigation Pane

  • Empty Navigation Pane: No headings are used - document will be impossible for screen readers to navigate efficiently
  • Multiple Heading 1s: Each appears as a separate top-level item - should only be one
  • Skipped levels: Heading 1 followed by Heading 3 - confusing hierarchy
  • Non-descriptive headings: "Section 1", "Part A" - not helpful for navigation
  • Missing headings: Large sections of text with no heading breaks - hard to navigate

Applying and modifying built-in heading styles

What it is

Word's built-in heading styles (Heading 1, Heading 2, Heading 3, etc.) are pre-configured text formats that serve two critical purposes:

  • Visual formatting: They make headings stand out with larger fonts, bold text, and spacing
  • Semantic structure: They tell assistive technology that text is a heading and what level it is

These styles are found in the Styles group on the Home tab and can be customized to match your organization's branding while maintaining their accessibility benefits.

Why it matters

Using built-in heading styles is essential for accessibility because:

  • Screen readers recognize them: Only text formatted with heading styles is announced as a heading to screen reader users
  • Manual formatting doesn't work: Making text large and bold doesn't make it a heading - it's just big text
  • Enables navigation: Screen readers let users jump between headings, but only if heading styles are used
  • Creates document structure: Heading styles build the document outline used by Navigation Pane and assistive technology
  • Supports PDF conversion: Properly styled headings in Word become properly tagged headings in PDFs
  • Maintains consistency: Using styles ensures consistent formatting throughout the document

❌ Common mistake: Manual formatting

Never create "headings" by just making text larger and bold. This looks like a heading visually but is completely invisible to screen readers. Always use the built-in heading styles.

How to do it

Applying heading styles

Method 1: Using the Ribbon

  1. Select the text you want to make a heading
  2. Go to the Home tab
  3. In the Styles group, click the heading style you want (Heading 1, Heading 2, etc.)
  4. If you don't see the style you need, click the More button (small arrow) in the Styles group to see all built-in styles

Method 2: Using keyboard shortcuts

  • Ctrl+Alt+1 = Apply Heading 1
  • Ctrl+Alt+2 = Apply Heading 2
  • Ctrl+Alt+3 = Apply Heading 3

Method 3: Using the Styles pane

  1. Press Ctrl+Alt+Shift+S to open the Styles pane
  2. Select your text
  3. Click the heading style in the Styles pane

Modifying heading styles to match your brand

You can customize how heading styles look while maintaining their accessibility:

  1. Right-click the style you want to modify in the Styles group or Styles pane
  2. Select Modify...
  3. In the Modify Style dialog, you can change:
    • Font family and size
    • Color
    • Bold, italic, underline
    • Spacing before and after
    • Indentation
  4. Click Format button for more options (paragraph, tabs, border, etc.)
  5. Check "Automatically update" if you want all instances to change when you modify one
  6. Choose whether to apply to "Only in this document" or "New documents based on this template"
  7. Click OK

⚠️ Important: Don't change the style names or hierarchy

You can change how Heading 1, Heading 2, etc. look, but:

  • Don't rename them to something else
  • Don't change their outline levels
  • Don't delete them

This ensures they maintain their semantic meaning for assistive technology.

Best practices for heading styles

  • Always use styles, never manual formatting: Select text and apply the heading style rather than manually making text large and bold
  • Use the correct hierarchy:
    • One Heading 1 (document title)
    • Heading 2 for main sections
    • Heading 3 for subsections under Heading 2
    • Continue logically without skipping levels
  • Make headings descriptive: "Financial Performance" is better than "Section 2"
  • Keep headings concise: Aim for short, scannable heading text
  • Use sentence case: "Financial performance" rather than "FINANCIAL PERFORMANCE"
  • Be consistent: If you modify styles, do it once and apply throughout the document

✅ Heading style checklist

  • ☐ All headings use built-in Heading styles (not manual formatting)
  • ☐ Heading hierarchy is logical (no skipped levels)
  • ☐ Only one Heading 1 per document
  • ☐ Headings are descriptive and meaningful
  • ☐ Style modifications maintain good contrast and readability
  • ☐ Navigation Pane shows clear, organized structure
