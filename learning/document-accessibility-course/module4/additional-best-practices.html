Handling abbreviations and acronyms in Word

What it is

Abbreviations and acronyms are shortened forms of words or phrases:

  • Abbreviation: Shortened form of a word or phrase (e.g., "Dr." for "Doctor", "etc." for "et cetera")
  • Acronym: Word formed from initial letters, usually pronounced as a word (e.g., "NASA", "SCUBA")
  • Initialism: Similar to acronym but pronounced letter-by-letter (e.g., "FBI", "HTML")

In Word documents, these shortened forms can cause confusion for:

  • Screen reader users who may not understand how to pronounce or expand them
  • People with cognitive disabilities who may struggle with abbreviations
  • Non-native language speakers unfamiliar with common abbreviations
  • Anyone encountering specialized or technical acronyms for the first time

Why it matters

Proper handling of abbreviations and acronyms is important because:

  • Screen reader pronunciation: Screen readers may mispronounce or spell out abbreviations in confusing ways
  • Comprehension: Unexplained acronyms create barriers to understanding, especially for specialized terminology
  • Cognitive accessibility: Clear, expanded text helps people with cognitive disabilities or memory challenges
  • International audiences: Not all abbreviations are universal; expanded forms help translation and understanding
  • Long documents: Readers may forget what an acronym means if they encounter it much later after the initial definition
  • Professional communication: Clearly defining terms demonstrates consideration for your audience

Example: Why definitions matter

Unclear: "The ITAO works with the SSC and the TBS to ensure WCAG compliance."

Clear: "The IT Accessibility Office (ITAO) works with Shared Services Canada (SSC) and the Treasury Board Secretariat (TBS) to ensure Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) compliance."

How to do it

Best practices for abbreviations and acronyms

1. Define on first use

The first time you use an abbreviation or acronym in your document, spell it out in full with the shortened form in parentheses:

  • Format: Full Term (Abbreviation)
  • Example: "The Information Technology Accessibility Office (ITAO) provides guidance..."
  • After the first definition, you can use the abbreviated form throughout the document

2. Consider your audience

  • Common abbreviations: Very well-known terms like "Mr.", "Dr.", "USA" may not need expansion
  • Specialized terms: Industry-specific or technical acronyms should always be defined
  • When in doubt: Define it - better to over-explain than leave readers confused

3. Use first occurrence in each major section

For very long documents:

  • Define acronyms on first use in the main document
  • Consider re-defining in major sections if the document is very long or complex
  • Definitely re-define if sections might be read independently

4. Create a glossary for documents with many terms

If your document uses many abbreviations or specialized terms:

  • Create an "Abbreviations" or "Glossary" section near the beginning or end
  • List terms alphabetically with their full expansions
  • Still define key terms on first use in the main text
  • Use proper heading styles for the glossary section

Handling specific types of abbreviations in Word

Common abbreviations that don't need expansion

  • Titles: Mr., Mrs., Ms., Dr.
  • Time designations: a.m., p.m., EST, GMT
  • Common Latin abbreviations: etc., e.g., i.e. (though consider writing them out for clarity)
  • Well-established geographic abbreviations: USA, UK, EU
  • Units of measurement: cm, km, lb (when used in technical context)

Technical and specialized abbreviations that need expansion

  • Industry-specific terms: ITAO, SSC, TBS, WCAG, AODA
  • Internal organizational acronyms
  • Technical standards and protocols
  • Program names and initiatives
  • Any term that might be unfamiliar to your audience

Plural abbreviations

  • Add "s" to make an abbreviation plural: PDFs, URLs, CPUs
  • Don't use apostrophes for plurals: ❌ "PDF's" ✅ "PDFs"
  • Expand on first use: "Portable Document Formats (PDFs)"

Word-specific techniques

Using Find and Replace to check abbreviations

  1. Press Ctrl+H to open Find and Replace
  2. Click More >> to expand options
  3. Check "Match case" to find all-caps acronyms
  4. Search for common patterns:
    • Two or more capital letters in a row (using wildcards)
    • Parentheses which often contain acronyms
  5. Review each found instance to ensure it's defined on first use

Using comments to track definitions

  • When you first define an acronym, add a comment to that paragraph
  • Comment could say "First use of ITAO defined here"
  • This helps during review to ensure definitions are present
  • Delete comments before finalizing the document

✅ Abbreviation and acronym checklist

  • ☐ All specialized acronyms are defined on first use
  • ☐ Definitions follow format: Full Term (Acronym)
  • ☐ Glossary provided if document uses many terms
  • ☐ Well-known abbreviations used appropriately
  • ☐ Terms redefined in separate major sections if needed
  • ☐ Document has been reviewed for undefined abbreviations

Examples of proper usage

Technical document example:

"The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 Level AA provide the standard for digital accessibility in Canada. WCAG covers four main principles: Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, and Robust (POUR). When implementing WCAG, organizations must ensure..."

Note: WCAG is defined on first use, then used throughout. POUR is also defined.

Glossary example:

Glossary of Terms

  • AODA: Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act
  • ITAO: IT Accessibility Office
  • PDF: Portable Document Format
  • SSC: Shared Services Canada
  • TBS: Treasury Board Secretariat
  • WCAG: Web Content Accessibility Guidelines

Managing audio/video content in Word documents

What it is

While Word is primarily for text-based documents, you can embed or link to multimedia content including:

  • Audio files (speeches, podcasts, sound clips)
  • Video files (training videos, recorded presentations, demonstrations)
  • Links to online multimedia content (YouTube videos, streaming audio)
  • Embedded media players

When including multimedia in Word documents, you must ensure it's accessible through captions, transcripts, and keyboard controls.

Why it matters

Accessible multimedia is critical because:

  • Deaf or hard of hearing users: Cannot access audio content without captions or transcripts
  • Blind or low vision users: Cannot access visual-only content without audio description
  • Cognitive disabilities: May need transcripts to review content at their own pace
  • Environmental constraints: Users in quiet spaces (libraries) or loud environments need alternatives to audio
  • Language barriers: Transcripts can be translated more easily than audio/video
  • Searchability: Text transcripts make multimedia content searchable
  • Bandwidth limitations: Text alternatives work when video streaming is not possible

⚠️ Important consideration

Embedding multimedia in Word documents can create large file sizes and compatibility issues. Consider carefully whether Word is the best format for distributing multimedia content. Often, hosting video online and providing a link is more accessible and practical.

How to do it

Best practices for multimedia in Word documents

1. Provide captions for video content

What it is

Captions are synchronized text that displays spoken dialogue, sound effects, and other relevant audio information on screen as the video plays.

Why it matters
  • Deaf users need captions to understand audio content
  • Required by accessibility standards (WCAG 2.1 Level AA)
  • Benefits all users in noisy environments or when audio is unavailable
How to do it
  • If embedding video in Word:
    • Videos must have built-in closed captions before embedding
    • Word cannot add captions to embedded videos
    • Create captions using video editing software before embedding
  • If linking to online video:
    • Ensure the video has captions on the hosting platform (YouTube, Vimeo, etc.)
    • Test that captions work correctly
    • Mention in the link text that captions are available: "Watch: Training Video (captioned)"

2. Provide transcripts for all multimedia

What it is

A transcript is a text version of all spoken content, audio descriptions, and relevant sound information from audio or video content.

Why it matters
  • Provides access for users who are deaf-blind
  • Allows users to read at their own pace
  • Makes content searchable
  • Supports translation and repurposing of content
  • Useful for users with slow internet connections
How to do it in Word

Method 1: Include transcript in the same document (Recommended)

  1. After the embedded video or link, add a heading: "Transcript"
  2. Use a Heading 2 or 3 style for the transcript heading
  3. Include the full text of all spoken content
  4. Identify speakers: "[Speaker name]: dialogue text"
  5. Include relevant sound effects: "[Sound effect: door closing]"
  6. Format transcript text clearly with proper paragraphs

Method 2: Link to a separate transcript document

  • Create transcript as a separate Word document
  • Ensure transcript document is also accessible
  • Add link in main document: "Download transcript (Word document)"
  • Make sure link is descriptive and accessible

✅ Good transcript example

Transcript: Product Launch Video

[Speaker 1 - Jane Smith, CEO]: Welcome to the launch of our new accessibility initiative. Today, we're excited to announce...

[Sound effect: Applause]

[Speaker 2 - John Doe, CTO]: The technical specifications include...

[Visual description: Screen shows product diagram with five key features highlighted]

3. Ensure keyboard controls for embedded media

What it is

Keyboard controls allow users to operate media players using only the keyboard (no mouse required).

Why it matters
  • Users with mobility disabilities may not be able to use a mouse
  • Screen reader users navigate by keyboard
  • Keyboard operation is often faster and more efficient
How to do it
  • Test embedded media with keyboard only (use Tab to navigate, Space or Enter to activate)
  • If media player doesn't support keyboard controls, provide external links to the media with accessible players
  • Document keyboard shortcuts if they're not standard (e.g., "Press Space to play/pause")

Recommended approaches for multimedia in Word

Option 1: Link to hosted video (Recommended)

  1. Upload video to an accessible platform (YouTube, Vimeo, organization's video hosting)
  2. Add captions to the video on the platform
  3. Create a full transcript
  4. In your Word document:
    • Provide descriptive link: "Watch: Annual Report Overview (5 minutes, captioned)"
    • Include the transcript in the Word document or provide link to transcript
    • Add a brief text description of what the video contains

Option 2: Embed video in Word

Note: Only use this approach for short videos in documents that will be used primarily digitally, not converted to PDF.

  1. Ensure video has captions built in (created in video editing software)
  2. Insert → Video → Video on My PC
  3. Add descriptive text before the video explaining what it contains
  4. Immediately after the video, provide a full transcript
  5. Test that video plays and controls work with keyboard
  6. Consider file size - embedded videos make documents very large

Option 3: Audio files

  1. Similar approach to video: link to hosted audio preferred
  2. Always provide a complete transcript
  3. Add description: "Listen: Podcast Interview with Dr. Smith (30 minutes)"
  4. Include transcript immediately following, or provide link to transcript document

Creating quality transcripts

For effective transcripts:

  • Include all spoken dialogue: Don't summarize or paraphrase
  • Identify speakers: Use names or roles in brackets
  • Include relevant audio information: Important sound effects, music, tone of voice when relevant
  • Describe important visual information: If video shows critical visual content, describe it
  • Use proper punctuation: Makes transcript easier to read
  • Break into paragraphs: Don't create one giant block of text
  • Include timestamps: For longer content, consider adding timestamps every few minutes

Special considerations

Audio descriptions for videos

If your video contains important visual information not conveyed through audio:

  • Create an audio-described version where a narrator describes visual content during pauses
  • Or ensure your transcript includes detailed descriptions of all important visual content
  • Note in your document that audio description is available if applicable

Autoplay

  • Never set multimedia to autoplay in Word documents
  • Users should control when media plays
  • Autoplay creates accessibility and usability problems

File format considerations

  • Use common, widely-supported formats (MP4 for video, MP3 for audio)
  • Test that media plays on different systems
  • Provide alternative formats if possible
  • Consider that Word documents with embedded media may not convert well to PDF

✅ Multimedia accessibility checklist

  • ☐ All video content has synchronized captions
  • ☐ Complete transcripts provided for all audio and video
  • ☐ Transcripts include speaker identification and relevant audio information
  • ☐ Media players can be operated with keyboard only
  • ☐ Descriptive text provided before multimedia explaining what it contains
  • ☐ Media does not autoplay
  • ☐ Links to multimedia use descriptive text
  • ☐ Audio descriptions provided for videos with important visual information
  • ☐ File sizes are reasonable for the document's intended use
