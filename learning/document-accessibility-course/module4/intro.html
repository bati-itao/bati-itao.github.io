What it is

Microsoft Word is the world's most widely used word processing application, serving as the primary tool for creating documents, reports, letters, manuals, and countless other text-based materials in organizations worldwide. While Module 2 covered general accessibility techniques that apply across all Microsoft 365 applications, Word has unique features, capabilities, and challenges that require specialized accessibility knowledge.

This module focuses specifically on Word's accessibility features and best practices, including:

  • Word-specific document structure and navigation tools
  • Table creation and formatting unique to Word
  • Managing complex visual content in Word documents
  • Word's built-in accessibility checker and review tools
  • Accessible templates and organizational standards
  • Word-specific considerations for abbreviations, acronyms, and multimedia

Why it matters

Word documents serve as foundational content throughout organizations and are often the source material for other formats like PDFs, web content, and presentations. Creating accessible Word documents is critical because:

Word documents are everywhere

  • Policy documents and procedures
  • Reports and briefing materials
  • Forms and templates
  • Training materials and manuals
  • Letters and correspondence
  • Meeting agendas and minutes

Word has unique accessibility challenges

  • Headers and footers: Content placed here may not be accessible to screen readers in the proper reading order
  • Complex tables: Word allows merged cells and nested tables that can be difficult for assistive technology to navigate
  • Navigation tools: Word's Navigation Pane and document structure features work differently than other applications
  • Long descriptions: Complex visuals may need detailed descriptions beyond simple alt text
  • Federal requirements: Government documents often require bilingual alt text for official images

Word documents become other formats

Word documents are frequently converted to:

  • PDFs for distribution and archiving
  • Web content through copy-and-paste or conversion tools
  • PowerPoint presentations
  • Email messages

When you create accessible Word documents, you build a strong foundation that helps maintain accessibility through these conversions. Fixing accessibility issues in Word is much easier than trying to remediate them in PDFs or other formats later.

Did you know?

Studies show that attempting to make an inaccessible PDF accessible can take 5-10 times longer than creating an accessible Word document from the start. Many PDF accessibility issues can be prevented by following proper Word accessibility techniques.

Word-specific techniques

You'll learn how to apply accessibility principles specifically in Word's environment, including:
