Why alternative text matters

Alternative text (alt text) describes images for people who cannot see them, including users of screen readers, people with visual impairments, and anyone in situations where images won't load or display properly.

In PowerPoint presentations, images often carry essential information that supports or enhances your message. Without proper alt text, this information becomes inaccessible to some users.

Who benefits from alt text

  • Screen reader users: Blind and visually impaired people who rely on assistive technology
  • People with cognitive disabilities: Who may process text better than complex images
  • Users with low bandwidth: Where images may not load properly
  • Search engines: Which index alt text to improve content discoverability
  • Voice interface users: Who interact with content through voice commands

Real-world impact

Consider a business presentation showing Q4 results. A chart displaying a 23% increase in sales might be the most important slide in the presentation. Without alt text, a screen reader user would only hear "image" and miss this crucial information entirely.

Good alt text example: "Bar chart showing Q4 sales increased 23% compared to Q3, from $2.1M to $2.6M, exceeding our target of $2.4M."

Legal and organizational requirements

  • Accessibility standards: WCAG 2.1 requires meaningful alt text for informative images
  • Government compliance: Public sector presentations must be accessible
  • Corporate policies: Many organizations have accessibility requirements
  • Inclusive design: Good alt text improves the experience for everyone

Types of images in presentations

Different types of images require different approaches to alt text. Understanding these categories helps you write appropriate descriptions.

Informative images

Carry essential information that supports your presentation content:

  • Screenshots: Demonstrating software features or processes
  • Product photos: Showing specific details or features
  • Maps and locations: Geographic or spatial information
  • Process diagrams: Workflows, organizational charts, or system architectures
  • Before/after comparisons: Showing changes or improvements

Alt text approach: Describe the essential information conveyed by the image.

Decorative images

Used for visual appeal but don't convey essential information:

  • Background patterns: Textures, gradients, or decorative elements
  • Stock photos: Used for mood or atmosphere without specific meaning
  • Decorative borders: Visual dividers that don't add information
  • Brand elements: Logos used purely for recognition (when context is clear)

Alt text approach: Mark as decorative so screen readers skip them.

Functional images

Act as buttons, links, or interactive elements:

  • Clickable icons: Navigation buttons or action triggers
  • Linked images: Photos or graphics that link to other content
  • Interactive elements: Hotspots or clickable areas

Alt text approach: Describe the action or destination, not the image appearance.

Complex images

Contain detailed information that requires extended description:

  • Charts and graphs: Data visualizations with multiple data points
  • Detailed diagrams: Technical illustrations or complex workflows
  • Infographics: Visual presentations of data or information
  • Screenshots with multiple elements: Complex interfaces or multi-step processes

Alt text approach: Provide both concise alt text and detailed long descriptions.

Writing effective alternative text

Key principles for good alt text

Be concise but complete

  • Aim for 125 characters or less when possible (screen reader buffer limit)
  • Include essential information but avoid unnecessary details
  • Focus on meaning, not visual description

Consider context

  • What role does the image play in your presentation?
  • What information would be lost if the image weren't there?
  • How does it relate to surrounding content?

Write for your audience

  • Use appropriate terminology for your audience's expertise level
  • Match the tone of your presentation
  • Include relevant details that support your message

Alt text writing guidelines

Do include

  • Purpose and meaning: Why the image is important
  • Key data points: Specific numbers or trends from charts
  • Essential text: Text that appears in images
  • Context clues: Information that helps understanding

Don't include

  • "Image of" or "Picture of": Screen readers announce it's an image
  • Purely visual details: Colors, fonts, or decorative elements (unless meaningful)
  • Redundant information: What's already explained in surrounding text
  • Subjective interpretations: Stick to factual descriptions

✅ Alt text quality check

Good alt text should:

  • Make sense when read aloud
  • Provide the same information as the image
  • Be appropriate for the presentation context
  • Help the listener understand your point

Common alt text patterns

For charts and graphs

Pattern: Chart type + data summary + key finding

Example: "Bar chart showing monthly sales from January to June 2024, with steady growth from $50K to $89K, peaking in June."

For screenshots

Pattern: Interface name + key elements + purpose

Example: "PowerPoint accessibility checker showing 3 errors: missing alt text on slide 2, poor color contrast on slide 5, and missing slide title on slide 8."

For process diagrams

Pattern: Process name + key steps + outcome

Example: "Workflow diagram showing document review process: draft creation, peer review, manager approval, and final publication."

PowerPoint 365 alt text features

PowerPoint 365 includes several advanced features to help create and manage alt text more efficiently.

AI-powered auto-generated alt text

PowerPoint can automatically generate alt text suggestions using artificial intelligence:

  • How it works: AI analyzes image content and generates descriptions
  • Activation: Enabled by default in PowerPoint 365
  • Quality: Good starting point but always review and refine
  • Limitations: May miss context, relationships, or specific details important to your presentation

⚠️ Always review auto-generated alt text

While AI-generated descriptions are helpful, they often need refinement:

  • Add context: Why the image matters in your presentation
  • Include specifics: Key data points or details the AI missed
  • Fix errors: AI may misinterpret image content
  • Match tone: Ensure language fits your presentation style

Alt Text Pane

The dedicated interface for managing alternative text:

Opening the Alt Text Pane

  1. Right-click on any image and select "Edit Alt Text"
  2. Or select image and go to Picture Format > Alt Text
  3. Or use Review > Check Accessibility and click on alt text issues

Alt Text Pane features

  • Description field: Where you write or edit alt text
  • Character counter: Shows length to help keep descriptions concise
  • "Mark as decorative" checkbox: For images that don't need alt text
  • Auto-generate button: Triggers AI to create description suggestions

Managing alt text at scale

Accessibility Checker integration

  • Automatic detection: Identifies images missing alt text
  • Quick access: Click on issues to open Alt Text Pane
  • Priority scoring: Shows which issues are most critical

Batch operations

  • Review all images: Use Accessibility Checker to see all alt text issues
  • Consistent naming: Develop patterns for similar images
  • Template approach: Create alt text templates for common image types

Advanced PowerPoint 365 features

Designer integration

  • Accessible suggestions: Designer considers alt text when suggesting layouts
  • Smart placement: Recommendations maintain good reading order
  • Context awareness: Suggestions based on existing content

Collaboration features

  • Comments on images: Team members can suggest alt text improvements
  • Review tracking: See who added or modified alt text
  • Version history: Track changes to alt text over time

Handling decorative images

Not all images need descriptive alt text. Decorative images should be marked to be ignored by screen readers to avoid unnecessary clutter.

Identifying decorative images

An image is decorative if removing it wouldn't change the meaning or function of your slide:

  • Background patterns or textures
  • Decorative borders or dividers
  • Stock photos used for visual appeal (not specific content)
  • Repetitive branding elements (when context is already clear)
  • Visual flourishes that don't convey information

Marking images as decorative in PowerPoint 365

  1. Select the decorative image
  2. Right-click and choose "Edit Alt Text"
  3. Check the "Mark as decorative" checkbox
  4. The description field will be disabled and cleared
  5. Screen readers will skip this image

💡 When in doubt, provide alt text

If you're unsure whether an image is decorative:

  • Ask: "Would removing this image change the meaning of my slide?"
  • Consider your audience's needs and expectations
  • Err on the side of inclusion - brief alt text is better than none
  • Test with actual users when possible

Common decorative image mistakes

Marking informative images as decorative

  • Problem: Important information becomes inaccessible
  • Example: A product photo marked as decorative in a sales presentation
  • Solution: Provide meaningful alt text for any image that supports your message

Not marking obvious decorative images

  • Problem: Screen readers announce meaningless content
  • Example: Background texture described as "abstract blue pattern"
  • Solution: Mark pure decorations as decorative to reduce noise

Complex images and long descriptions

Some images contain too much information for concise alt text. These require both brief alt text and detailed long descriptions.

When to provide long descriptions

  • Detailed charts or graphs with multiple data series
  • Complex diagrams with many interconnected elements
  • Infographics combining text, images, and data
  • Technical illustrations showing detailed processes or structures
  • Screenshots of complex interfaces with multiple elements

Two-part approach: Alt text + long description

Brief alt text (125 characters or less)

Provides a concise summary of the image's purpose and key finding:

Example: "Organizational chart showing company hierarchy with CEO, 4 VPs, and 12 department managers."

Detailed long description

Provides comprehensive information about the image content:

Example in slide content: "The organizational chart shows Sarah Johnson as CEO at the top, with four Vice Presidents reporting to her: Marketing (Mike Chen), Operations (Lisa Rodriguez), Finance (David Kim), and Technology (Anna Patel). Under each VP are 3 department managers responsible for specific areas..."

Methods for providing long descriptions

Option 1: Include in slide content

  • Best for: Information that benefits all users
  • Method: Add detailed description as text on the same slide or next slide
  • Advantage: Everyone can access the detailed information

Option 2: Speaker notes

  • Best for: Information you'll speak aloud during presentation
  • Method: Add detailed description to slide notes
  • Advantage: Doesn't clutter slide design
  • Note: Only accessible if notes are shared or read aloud

Option 3: Appendix or data tables

  • Best for: Raw data underlying charts and graphs
  • Method: Include data tables at end of presentation
  • Advantage: Provides exact values for detailed analysis

✅ Long description best practices

  • Start with overview: Describe the general structure or purpose
  • Follow logical order: Describe elements in a systematic way
  • Include key relationships: How elements connect or compare
  • Highlight important findings: What conclusions should be drawn
  • Provide context: How this relates to your main message

Icons and symbols

Icons and symbols are common in PowerPoint presentations but require special consideration for alt text.

Types of icons and symbols

Functional icons

Icons that represent actions or navigation:

  • Navigation arrows: Next, previous, home
  • Action buttons: Play, pause, download
  • Social media icons: Links to social platforms

Alt text approach: Describe the action, not the appearance

Example: "Next slide" not "right arrow"

Informational icons

Icons that convey specific meaning:

  • Status indicators: Success, warning, error
  • Category markers: Different types of content
  • Rating systems: Stars, thumbs up/down

Alt text approach: Describe the meaning

Example: "Warning" not "triangle with exclamation mark"

Decorative icons

Icons used purely for visual appeal:

  • Bullet point replacements: Decorative list markers
  • Visual flourishes: Design elements
  • Redundant icons: When text already explains the concept

Alt text approach: Mark as decorative

Icon font considerations

When using icon fonts (like Font Awesome or similar):

  • Ensure proper markup: Icons should be marked with appropriate roles
  • Provide text alternatives: Use aria-label or visually hidden text
  • Test with screen readers: Verify icons are announced correctly

Cultural and international considerations

  • Universal symbols: Prefer widely recognized icons
  • Cultural sensitivity: Some symbols have different meanings in different cultures
  • Text backup: Consider including text labels alongside icons
  • Color independence: Don't rely only on color to convey icon meaning

Practical examples

Business chart examples

❌ Poor alt text

  • "Chart"
  • "Sales data"
  • "Bar graph showing information"
  • "Image of quarterly results"

✅ Good alt text

  • "Bar chart showing Q4 sales increased 15% to $2.3M"
  • "Line graph showing customer satisfaction improved from 3.2 to 4.1 stars over 6 months"
  • "Pie chart showing marketing budget: 40% digital, 35% traditional, 25% events"

Screenshot examples

❌ Poor alt text

  • "Screenshot"
  • "Computer screen"
  • "Software interface"

✅ Good alt text

  • "PowerPoint ribbon showing Insert tab with Icons button highlighted"
  • "Email compose window with accessibility checker showing 2 warnings"
  • "Dashboard displaying 5 active projects, 3 completed, 2 overdue"

Product photo examples

❌ Poor alt text

  • "Product photo"
  • "Laptop computer"
  • "Picture of device"

✅ Good alt text

  • "Silver laptop with 15-inch screen showing new design interface"
  • "Smartphone displaying mobile app with accessibility features enabled"
  • "Wireless headphones with noise-canceling controls on left ear cup"

Alt text quality checklist

  • ☐ Every informative image has meaningful alt text
  • ☐ Alt text describes purpose and key information, not appearance
  • ☐ Complex images have both alt text and long descriptions
  • ☐ Decorative images are marked as decorative
  • ☐ Functional images describe the action, not the icon
  • ☐ Alt text is concise but complete (under 125 characters when possible)
  • ☐ Language matches presentation tone and audience level
  • ☐ AI-generated alt text has been reviewed and refined
  • ☐ No images have empty alt text unless truly decorative
  • ☐ Alt text provides same information value as the visual
