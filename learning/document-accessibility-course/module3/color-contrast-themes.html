Why color contrast matters

Adequate color contrast ensures that text and visual elements are readable by everyone, including people with visual impairments, color vision differences, and those viewing presentations in challenging lighting conditions.

Poor contrast affects many people:

  • Visual impairments: People with low vision need high contrast to distinguish text from background
  • Color vision differences: About 8% of men and 0.5% of women have some form of color blindness
  • Environmental factors: Bright rooms, projector limitations, or mobile devices can reduce visible contrast
  • Age-related changes: Vision naturally changes with age, affecting contrast sensitivity
  • Temporary impairments: Eye fatigue, medical conditions, or medications can affect vision

Real-world scenarios

  • A presentation in a bright conference room where projector contrast is poor
  • Someone reviewing slides on their phone during their commute
  • A team member with color vision differences who can't distinguish red from green status indicators
  • An older colleague who has difficulty reading light gray text on white backgrounds

Good contrast design benefits everyone in these situations.

Contrast requirements and guidelines

WCAG contrast standards

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) provide specific contrast ratio requirements:

Normal text (AA level):
4.5:1 contrast ratio minimum for text smaller than 18pt regular or 14pt bold
Large text (AA level):
3:1 contrast ratio minimum for text 18pt+ regular or 14pt+ bold
Enhanced contrast (AAA level):
7:1 for normal text, 4.5:1 for large text (higher standard, recommended when possible)
Non-text elements:
3:1 contrast ratio for graphics, icons, and interface elements

Understanding contrast ratios

Contrast ratios represent the difference in brightness between foreground and background:

  • 1:1 = No contrast (same color)
  • 3:1 = Minimum for large text and graphics
  • 4.5:1 = Minimum for normal text
  • 7:1 = Enhanced contrast
  • 21:1 = Maximum contrast (black on white)

Government of Canada requirements

ESDC follows Government of Canada digital standards:

  • All presentations must meet WCAG 2.1 AA standards
  • This includes the 4.5:1 contrast requirement for regular text
  • Consider AAA levels (7:1) for critical information
  • Test all color combinations before using them

Using PowerPoint themes for accessibility

Choose high-contrast themes

PowerPoint includes several built-in themes designed with good contrast:

  • Office theme: Classic white background with dark text
  • Facet theme: Clean design with strong contrast
  • Ion theme: Dark background option with light text
  • Retrospect theme: High contrast with professional appearance

Customizing theme colors

To modify theme colors for better accessibility:

  1. Go to Design > Variants > Colors
  2. Choose "Customize Colors" at the bottom
  3. Select colors with adequate contrast ratios
  4. Test combinations before applying
  5. Save as a custom color scheme

Government of Canada theme compliance

When using official GoC branding:

  • Ensure brand colors meet contrast requirements
  • Use approved color combinations from brand guidelines
  • Test all custom colors for accessibility
  • Consider creating accessible variants of brand themes

Creating accessible custom themes

When developing custom themes:

  1. Start with high contrast: Use dark text on light backgrounds or vice versa
  2. Test all combinations: Check every color pairing you plan to use
  3. Provide alternatives: Create both light and dark theme options
  4. Document ratios: Keep track of which combinations meet standards

Testing color contrast

PowerPoint's built-in accessibility checker

PowerPoint can identify some contrast issues:

  1. Go to Review > Check Accessibility
  2. Look for "Hard-to-read text contrast" warnings
  3. Address flagged issues by changing colors
  4. Note: The checker may not catch all contrast problems

External contrast testing tools

For more accurate testing, use dedicated tools:

WebAIM Contrast Checker (free, online)

  • Enter hex color codes or use color picker
  • Shows exact contrast ratios
  • Indicates AA and AAA compliance
  • URL: webaim.org/resources/contrastchecker

Colour Contrast Analyser (free desktop app)

  • Eyedropper tool for testing any colors on screen
  • Real-time contrast ratio calculation
  • WCAG compliance indicators
  • Available for Windows and Mac

Browser extensions

  • WAVE browser extension
  • Lighthouse accessibility audits
  • axe DevTools

Manual testing methods

Quick visual tests to supplement tools:

  • Squint test: Squint at your slides - can you still read the text?
  • Grayscale test: Convert slides to grayscale - is information still clear?
  • Distance test: View slides from across the room
  • Lighting test: Check slides in bright and dim lighting

Designing for color vision differences

Understanding color vision differences

Common types of color vision differences:

Protanopia/Protanomaly:
Difficulty distinguishing red from green (most common)
Deuteranopia/Deuteranomaly:
Also affects red-green perception but differently
Tritanopia/Tritanomaly:
Difficulty with blue-yellow distinctions (less common)
Monochromacy:
Complete color blindness (very rare)

Avoiding problematic color combinations

Color pairs that often cause problems:

  • Red and green: Classic problem combination
  • Orange and red: Often indistinguishable
  • Blue and purple: Difficult for some to differentiate
  • Light colors: Pink, light green, light blue, light gray

Safe color strategies

Color combinations that work well for most people:

  • High contrast pairs: Black/white, dark blue/white, white/dark gray
  • Blue and orange: Opposite on color wheel, good contrast
  • Blue and yellow: Strong contrast, widely accessible
  • Purple and yellow: Good contrast and differentiation

Testing for color vision differences

Tools to simulate different types of color vision:

  • Coblis Color Blindness Simulator: Upload images to see how they appear
  • Sim Daltonism: Mac app for real-time color blindness simulation
  • Photoshop: View > Proof Setup > Color Blindness options
  • Chrome extensions: Various colorblind simulation tools

Beyond color: Using multiple indicators

Don't rely on color alone

Never use color as the only way to convey information. Always provide additional visual cues that don't depend on color perception.

Alternative visual indicators

  • Shapes: Use different shapes for different categories
  • Patterns: Add patterns, textures, or hatching
  • Typography: Use bold, italic, or different fonts
  • Icons: Add symbols to reinforce meaning
  • Position: Use layout to convey relationships
  • Size: Vary size to show importance or data values

Practical design tips

Text and background combinations

  • Dark text on light backgrounds: Easier to read for most people
  • White text on dark backgrounds: Can work but test carefully
  • Avoid reverse text: White text on colored backgrounds often fails contrast
  • Test with various text sizes: Large text can get away with lower contrast

Working with brand colors

When brand colors don't meet contrast requirements:

  • Darken or lighten: Adjust brand colors slightly for accessibility
  • Use as accents: Reserve brand colors for decorative elements
  • Add borders: Use borders to separate colors that are too similar
  • Test variations: Find accessible shades within the brand palette

Image and graphic considerations

  • Text over images: Add semi-transparent overlays for contrast
  • Chart colors: Choose distinct colors and add patterns
  • Icon design: Ensure icons have adequate contrast with backgrounds
  • Map graphics: Don't rely on color alone to show different regions

Presentation environment factors

  • Projector limitations: Colors appear less saturated on projectors
  • Room lighting: Bright rooms wash out low-contrast content
  • Screen differences: Colors vary between different displays
  • Mobile viewing: Small screens and outdoor viewing reduce contrast

Good and bad examples

Example 1: Text contrast

❌ Poor contrast

Light gray text on light background (1.6:1 ratio)

Light blue text on blue background (2.1:1 ratio)

✅ Good contrast

Dark text on white background (12.6:1 ratio)

White text on dark background (15.3:1 ratio)

Example 2: Status indicators

❌ Color only

  • Project Alpha
  • Project Beta
  • Project Gamma

Users with color vision differences can't distinguish status

✅ Multiple indicators

  • ✓ Completed: Project Alpha
  • ⏳ In Progress: Project Beta
  • ⚠ Delayed: Project Gamma

Icons and text labels reinforce the color coding

Example 3: Chart accessibility

❌ Similar colors only

  • Red line for Sales
  • Green line for Profit
  • Orange line for Expenses

Lines may be indistinguishable for some users

✅ Color plus patterns

  • Blue solid line for Sales
  • Orange dashed line for Profit
  • Gray dotted line for Expenses

Different line styles work even without color

✅ Color and contrast checklist

  • All text meets minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio
  • Large text (18pt+) meets 3:1 contrast ratio
  • Non-text elements meet 3:1 contrast ratio
  • Information isn't conveyed by color alone
  • Color combinations work for color vision differences
  • Tested with contrast checking tools
  • Checked in different lighting conditions
  • Verified on different devices/projectors
