Why it matters

Adequate contrast and non-reliance on color alone benefit many users. People with visual impairments, color vision deficiencies, or those viewing presentations in various lighting conditions all need sufficient contrast to read content effectively.

Who benefits from good contrast?

  • People with low vision - Need high contrast to see text clearly
  • People with color blindness - Cannot distinguish certain color combinations
  • Older adults - May have declining vision that makes low contrast difficult
  • Everyone in challenging lighting - Bright rooms, dim rooms, or outdoor settings
  • Mobile device users - Small screens and varied viewing conditions

Practical guidance

  1. Use theme colors designed for contrast; test text/background pairs to meet 4.5:1 (normal text) or 3:1 (≥ 18 pt / bold)
  2. Ensure non-text elements (icons, lines) meet 3:1 against adjacent colors
  3. Do not use color alone to denote meaning; pair color with labels or patterns

WCAG contrast requirements:

Content Type AA Standard AAA Standard (Enhanced)
Normal text (under 18pt) 4.5:1 7:1
Large text (18pt+ or 14pt+ bold) 3:1 4.5:1
Non-text elements (icons, charts) 3:1 4.5:1

Testing contrast ratios

Recommended tools:

  • WebAIM Contrast Checker - Free online tool
  • Colour Contrast Analyser - Desktop application
  • PowerPoint Accessibility Checker - Built-in tool
  • Stark (browser extension) - For web-based checking

Testing process:

  1. Identify foreground and background colors
  2. Use contrast checker to verify ratio
  3. Test all color combinations in your theme
  4. Document approved combinations for team use
  5. Retest when making design changes

Beyond color coding

Alternative methods to convey information:

Visual methods:

  • Patterns and textures in charts
  • Shape differences for data points
  • Line styles (solid, dashed, dotted)
  • Icons and symbols alongside color

Text-based methods:

  • Direct labeling of chart elements
  • Legends with descriptions
  • Written explanations of color coding
  • Data tables as alternatives

Common scenarios:

Charts and graphs:

  • Use patterns + colors for different data series
  • Include direct labels on chart elements
  • Provide data table alongside visual chart

Status indicators:

  • Green checkmark = "Complete" (not just green)
  • Red X = "Failed" (not just red)
  • Yellow triangle = "Warning" (not just yellow)

Quick checklist

Before finalizing color choices:

  • ☐ All text meets contrast minimums (4.5:1 normal, 3:1 large)
  • ☐ Color is never the only channel for information
  • ☐ Non-text elements meet 3:1 contrast ratio
  • ☐ Theme colors tested and documented
  • ☐ Alternative methods provided for color-coded information
  • ☐ Charts include patterns or direct labels beyond color
